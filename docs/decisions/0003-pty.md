# 0003: PTY implementation — pty-process

- Date: 2026-07-11
- Status: accepted

## Context

The PTY backend must support Linux production and macOS development, integrate
with Tokio, propagate resize, bound output, and guarantee that a disconnect
terminates and reaps the child and its descendants. Chunk 0.3 compared
portable-pty, pty-process, and direct nix/rustix ownership.

## Decision

Use pty-process with its `async` feature for v0.1. Wrap it in ttygate's own
session abstraction rather than exposing the crate API beyond the execution
backend.

Lifecycle policy is part of this decision: spawn the target as the PTY session
leader; retain its PID/process-group identity; on close or WebSocket drop stop
I/O, signal the process group, wait for a short bounded grace period, escalate
to SIGKILL if necessary, and always await `Child::wait` to reap it. Output goes
through a bounded Tokio channel and cancellation remains selectable while a
send is backpressured.

## Alternatives

- **portable-pty 0.9.0:** capable, maintained, and the choice if Windows ConPTY
  becomes a requirement. For v0.1 it adds cross-platform and serial abstraction
  not needed by the Linux/macOS scope, while its Unix reads require a blocking
  adapter.
- **Direct nix/rustix:** viable and gives exact control. The experiment required
  ttygate-owned unsafe pre-exec and ioctl contracts plus descriptor, session,
  controlling-terminal, nonblocking-I/O, signal, and wait policy. That is
  security-sensitive surface without a product requirement that justifies it.

## Evidence

The disposable program in `spikes/pty/` ran all three approaches against the
same real fixture. Each observed resize inside the child and killed/reaped both
the session leader and a long-lived descendant. pty-process completed 20/20
repetitions. Its async PTY implements Tokio I/O, resize is explicit, and the
spawned child is a Tokio Child with kill-on-drop, kill, and wait support.

The dated comparison of platform, dependency, unsafe-ownership, resize,
teardown, async/backpressure, and testability evidence is in
`spikes/evidence/pty.md`. pty-process currently resolves through rustix; direct
lockfile/dependency data was inspected with `cargo tree`.

## Risks and mitigations

- pty-process is Unix-only. That matches v0.1; repeat lifecycle tests on Linux
  CI and reconsider portable-pty only if Windows becomes an actual target.
- Dropping a child handle is not reaping, and killing only the immediate PID can
  orphan descendants. Chunk 1.4 must own process-group signal plus mandatory
  wait and prove both child and descendant absence.
- PTY EOF and platform error codes vary. Treat reader termination as a lifecycle
  event and key correctness to child status/reaping, not one errno string.
- Backpressure can block an I/O task. Use bounded channels with cancellation in
  the same select loop and test an output flood.
- A small upstream raises continuity risk; its refs were reachable during the
  spike but a full clone hit an HTTP 503 on an older tag object. Keep the
  wrapper narrow, pin the crate, and retain the direct experiment as evidence
  that replacement with rustix is feasible.

## Consequences

Chunk 1.4 can design its async Session handle around pty-process, bounded Tokio
channels, and explicit process-group teardown. The spike itself remains outside
the production workspace.
